I met a friend's partner this evening. She's an Executive Assistant and conversation over drinks turned to AI.

Over the last few months I've found it increasingly hard to talk about AI with people who aren't programmers (or adjacent) because the delta between *AI as used for coding* and *AI as used by most people* is getting bigger every day. The reasons for this are kinda obvious, but the effect is this:

> For programmers, AI Tools have taken away much of the boring and mundane work and left humans with the interesting, creative bits. For most other people they have done exactly the opposite.

Good programmers aren't good because they're skilled at typing code, just as good EAs aren't good because they're skilled at  managing diaries or booking flights. Being able to do those things is an annoying-but-nececarry distraction from the **actual** work of:

- Understanding people and how they interact – as they **actually are**, not as they "should" be.
- Understanding & creating systems to **help** people, both now and into the future.
- Making correct decisions with insufficient information (often with no more than guesses & vibes) without recourse to others.
- Accepting and welcoming change TK

I've met some incredible EA's over the years, most of which share a few things in common:

- They are 
- They outsource everything which can be done more efficiently by someone else, whether to software or external service providers.  (eg. the Amex concierge travel team are always gonna be better at complex, weird travel plans because they're experts with a million contacts).
- They don't outsource things they ar best-placed to do.
- They can call their boss an arse and be listened to. Because mutual respect.
- Their boss will move heaven and earth to keep them there.
